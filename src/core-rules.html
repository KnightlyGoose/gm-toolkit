Core Rules

What Is ARES?

ARES is a tabletop roleplaying game. Players take on the roles of characters in a shared fictional world, describing what their characters say, do, and attempt.

One player acts as the Game Master (GM). The GM describes the world, controls the people and creatures the player characters encounter, and decides how the world responds to the players' actions.

Most of the time, players simply describe what their characters want to do. When the outcome is uncertain, difficult, dangerous, or opposed, the GM may call for a skill check.

Players usually roll for their own characters. The GM rolls for the environment, enemies, animals, bystanders, and other non-player characters.

The GM may also roll in secret when the player characters would not logically know whether they succeeded or failed, such as when searching for a hidden trap, sneaking past a guard, or trying to detect a lie.

What you need to play

To play, you will need three six-sided dice (d6), a pen or pencil, and paper.

This game uses a shorthand called Dice Notation.

THe number before the letter "d" tells you how many dice to roll. The number after the letter "d" tells you how many sides those dice have.

For example 3d6 means to roll three six-sided dice and add the results.

ARES uses exclusively six-sided dice.

Skill Checks

When a character attempts to do something uncertain or difficult, the Game Master(Gm) may call for a skill check.

Skill checks should only be called for when failure is possible and meaningful. Ordinary tasks do not require a roll.

Combat checks always require a roll.

Rolling a Skill Check

Roll 3d6 and compare to the result of your relevant skill. In the case of normal checks, a roll lower than your skill results in a success. For opposed rolls, compare the results of skill-3d6 between the two parties.

Advantage and Disadvantage

Certain situations may grant Advantage or Disadvantage on a skill check.

For each stack of Advantage, roll one additional die and discard the highest result.

For each stack of Disadvantage, roll one additional die and discard the lowest result.

After discarding dice, total the remaining three dice as normal.

Difficulty

The difficulty of a skill check is measured using Advantage and Disadvantage

Difficulty Roll
Simple No roll required. If multiple sources of advantage would apply, the check is considred simple
Favorable Roll with Advnatage: 4d6, dropping the highest die
Standard Roll normally: 3d6
Difficult Roll with Disadvantage: 4d6, dropping the lowest die
Extreme Roll with two Disadvantages: 5d6, dropping the two lowest dice.
Impossible If three or more sources of Disadvantage would apply, the check is considered Impossible and cannot be attempted.

Opposed Checks

Regular skill checks are used when a character acts against an inanimate object, an environment, or an abstract situation.

If another person, creature, or animal is directly opposing the character, the check is an opposed check.

In an opposed check, both parties roll using the relevant skill. Each party subtracts their roll from their skill. The party with the higher result wins.

Contests

Some opposed situations require more than one roll or more than one skill. These extended opposed checks are called contests.

During a contest, each side makes the required checks and tracks their number of successes. The side with the greater number of successes wins the contest.

Contests are similar to combat, but the length of each turn depends on the situation. A combat turn represents one second, while a contest turn might represent seconds, minutes, days, or even weeks.

For example, a chess match might use contest turns that represent several minutes of play. A hacker trying to break through a heavily encrypted system while a security team works against them might use contest turns that represent hours, days, or weeks.

Critical Successes and Critical Failures

Outside of combat, contests, and opposed checks, critical successes and critical failures are automatic successes or automatic failures.

The threshold for a critical success or critical failure is based on the character's skill.

The critical failure threshold is found with the following formula:

min(18, 15 + (skill - 7) / 3)

The critical success threshold is found with the following formula:

3 + (skill - 7) / 3

Specific critical thresholds are shown in the table below.

Critical Success Thresholds

Skill Critical Success
Below 7 No critical success possible
7–9 3
10–12 3–4
13–15 3–5
16+ 3–6

Critical Failure Thresholds

Skill Critical Failure
Below 7 15+
7–9 16+
10–12 17+
13+ 18

Critical success and critical failure thresholds depend on the skill being rolled. Higher skills make critical successes more likely and critical failures less likely.

Critical Results in Opposed Checks and Contests

In opposed checks and contests, critical results do not automatically succeed or fail. Instead, they change the final score of the roll.

On a critical success, the character's score is equal to their skill.

On a critical failure, the character subtracts their roll from their skill as normal, then subtracts one additional die.

Core Attributes

Every character is defined by four core attributes. These attributes measure a character's natural abilities and determine many of their derived statistics and skills.

Strength (STR)

Strength represents a character's overall physical power, fitness, and endurance.

Strength determines maximum health, melee damage, running speed, stamina, and carrying capacity.

Strength is used whenever a character must rely on raw physical force or bodily conditioning. This includes actions such as breaking down doors, forcing jars open, carrying heavy loads, climbing, wrestling, or controlling the recoil of automatic weapons.

Reflexes (REF)

Reflexes measures reaction speed, coordination, and balance.

Reflexes is used whenever a character must react quickly or maintain precise control over their movements. It is commonly used for dodging attacks, recovering from falls, keeping balance on unstable surfaces, or catching moving objects.

Intelligence Quotient (IQ)

IQ measures pattern recognition, logical reasoning, and analytical thinking. It does not measure education, memory, wisdom, or general knowledge.

IQ is not normally rolled on its own. Instead, it is used to grant Advantage to other skill checks when analysis, prediction, or reasoning would be helpful.

For example, IQ may grant Advantage when analyzing encrypted data, predicting an opponent's movement, identifying patterns, calculating trajectories, or planning complex actions.

Willpower (WIL)

Willpower measures mental endurance, focus, discipline, and resistance to stress.

Willpower is used whenever a character must continue functioning under pain, exhaustion, fear, distraction, or emotional pressure.

This includes resisting intimidation, maintaining concentration, staying awake through exhaustion, ignoring pain, or forcing oneself back to their feet after suffering a heavy blow.

Derived Statistics

Derived statistics are secondary values calculated from a character's attributes. These values represent a character's physical durability, movement ability, and carrying capacity.

Maximum Health (HP)

A character's maximum Health Points (HP) are equal to their Strength (STR).

Speed

Speed measures how quickly a character can move and react in combat.

Speed is calculated using the following formula:

(STR + REF + HP) / 4

Speed is not rounded.

Speed is calculated using the character's current HP, not their maximum HP. As a character takes damage, their Speed is reduced.

Dodge

Dodge represents a character's ability to avoid attacks through movement, reflexes, and positioning.

Dodge is calculated using the following formula:

floor(Speed) + (REF - 10) - Encumbrance

Dodge is reduced by Encumbrance.

Carry Weight

A character's carry weight determines how much equipment and gear they can carry without penalty.

Carry weight is equal to:

STR × 8

Encumbrance

Encumbrance represents the penalties caused by carrying excessive weight.

A character gains one Encumbrance counter for every one-fifth of their maximum carry weight currently carried.

Encumbrance reduces Dodge and may apply additional penalties at the GM's discretion.

Skills

Skills represent learned and practiced abilities. Skills cover a wide range of disciplines, from weapon use and lockpicking to programming, medicine, and piloting.

The GM is expected to create or modify skills to fit the setting and style of the game. Example skill lists are provided elsewhere in the rules.

General Skills and Specializations

Many skills can be divided into more focused specializations. General skills represent broad training, while specializations represent expertise in a narrow field.

A specialization is required for any skill level of 14 or higher.

When using a specialization, the related general skill is always two points lower.

For example, a master swordsman with Sword 14 would have Melee 12. A hacker with Hacking 15 would have Computer 13.

Combat Basics

These rules cover the basics needed to run combat. For advanced rules, including armor, limb damage, and optional systems, see the full Combat Rules.

Combat actions use the standard skill check and opposed check rules described above.

Turn Order

Turn order is determined by each character’s Speed. Higher Speed acts first. Speed is not rounded for this purpose.

If two characters have the same Speed, the character with the higher Reflexes (REF) acts first. If there is still a tie, both roll a die. The lower result acts first.

Actions

At the start of each turn, a character has five effective actions: one Step and four Actions.

A Step allows a character to move one yard and does not cost an Action.

Actions are the primary resource in combat. They are used for all major activities, including movement, attacking, aiming, reloading, and defending.

Each Action also allows a character to move up to their Speed in feet.

Actions may be saved and used to defend against attacks. A character who spends all of their Actions during their turn will be unable to defend themselves effectively.

Repeated Actions

Repeating the same type of action within a single turn becomes progressively harder.

Each additional use of the same action applies one stack of Disadvantage. For example, defending against a second attack in the same turn is made with Disadvantage, and a third defense is made with two stacks of Disadvantage.

Melee Attacks

Melee attacks are resolved as opposed checks between the attacker and the defender.

The attacker rolls using the relevant weapon skill. The defender may respond with a Dodge or a Parry.

Dodging uses the Dodge statistic. Parrying uses the appropriate weapon or shield skill. Shields grant Advantage when used to parry in melee.

The character with the higher result succeeds.

Ranged Attacks

Ranged attacks are resolved as standard skill checks.

If the attacker succeeds, the attack hits. If the attacker fails, the attack misses.

Some slow-moving projectiles, such as thrown weapons, may be reacted to at the GM’s discretion. Fast projectiles, such as bullets, cannot normally be reacted to.

Damage

When an attack hits, roll 1d6 to determine the quality of the hit. Multiply the result by the weapon’s damage type multiplier.

Damage Type Multiplier
Unarmed ×0.5
Blunt ×1
Slashing ×1.5 vs limbs, ×0.5 vs HP
Piercing ×1.5 vs HP, ×0.5 vs limbs
Ballistic ×4

Damage reduces the target’s HP. As HP decreases, a character’s Speed and Dodge are also reduced.

Death and Dying

Reaching 0 HP does not immediately kill a character.

When a character is reduced to 0 HP or lower, they must make a Willpower (WIL) check at the start of each of their turns to remain conscious and continue acting.

If the check is failed, the character collapses and can no longer take actions.

At HP equal to negative their maximum HP (−Max HP), this Willpower check is made with Disadvantage.

At HP equal to twice negative their maximum HP (−2 × Max HP), the character may make one final Willpower check. Regardless of the result, they collapse after this turn.

If not stabilized and treated, a character at this level of injury will die within hours.

At HP equal to three times negative their maximum HP (−3 × Max HP), the character dies immediately.
